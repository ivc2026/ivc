Provable Speech Attributes Conversion
via Latent Independence

Anonymous
Anonymous

Model

Indepedence-based Voice Conversion (IVC) consists of an encoder predicting content-like latent variable Ŝ and a decoder conditioned on speaker, emotion, or loudness embeddings C. The model is trained with discrepancy loss R and independence loss I

Highlights

  • Provides theoretical guarantees: recovering the latent variable up to an invertible map is sufficient to guarantee correct conversion.
  • Derives an explicit conversion error bound under imperfect reconstruction & independence.
  • Simplified training: trains using only two MSE losses — no GANs, no VQ codebooks, no adversarial training.
  • Supports speaker, emotion, [pitch] and loudness conversion — individually or jointly.
  • Performs one-shot inference from a single reference utterance (≥ 3 seconds) .
  • Linear-time inference, improving KNN-VC’s O(n²) complexity.
  • Achieves competitive or better results vs. KNN-VC, Free-VC, and emotion conversion baselines.

Abstract

Signal conversion and disentangled representation learning are central to controlled generation across audio, vision, and multimodal domains. However, despite strong empirical progress, particularly in speech style conversion, most existing approaches rely on heuristic objectives and architectural choices, offering limited theoretical understanding of when and why reliable attribute control is achievable. In this work, we develop a formal framework for speech attribute conversion and provide a theoretical analysis of the conditions under which exact and consistent conversion is possible. Our analysis focuses on a deterministic autoencoder setting augmented with an independence constraint between the learned latent representation and the controllable attribute. Under mild and interpretable assumptions on the data-generating process, we establish guarantees linking reconstruction, independence, and the feasibility of attribute manipulation while preserving task-relevant content. We complement the theoretical results with empirical evaluations on speech conversion tasks involving speaker identity and emotion, demonstrating that models aligned with the proposed framework exhibit the predicted behaviors in practice. These experiments primarily validate the theoretical insights and show that the proposed framework achieves competitive performance in practical speech conversion settings.

Loudness conversion

We present conversion examples corresponding to audio pairs where the mean loudness difference between the source and reference signals exceeds 10 dB, ensuring that the selected samples represent substantial loudness variation. In the proposed framework, loudness serves as an additional conditioning variable for the decoder, alongside speaker identity and emotion, enabling fine-grained control over expressive intensity during synthesis.
